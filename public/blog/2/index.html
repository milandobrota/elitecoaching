Pain Is a Teacher


Photo by: Ship Factory

You feel pain for a reason: Your body is letting you know that something needs to be changed. It makes you aware of a problem.

Often times, the pain teaches you a lesson.

If you try to avoid pain, you may never learn the lesson. I'm not talking just about physical pain. Emotional pain can be avoided as well, by distracting yourself or using drugs or alcohol.

Sometimes people learn the wrong lesson. I tore the ACL in my right knee playing basketball - maybe I should've never played? If a woman gets cheated on, are all men unfaithful? If you are not happy with your past, should you give up on your future?

Pain can make you a better person. You may know some great people who experienced a lot of pain in their lives. It is much easier for you to understand how another person feels if you've felt that way before. Be careful, though, every situation is different - don't assume too much too early.

The saying goes: "If it doesn't kill you, it makes you stronger." If you've experienced a tremendeous amount of pain in the past, the present and future might look less scary, because the new pain will be insignificant in comparison to the old pain.

The alternative is to get scared of the additional pain or start feeling helpless. You decide.

Pain can be a very strong driving force. Have you ever said to yourself (or heard someone else say) in frustration: "I've had enough!" At that point, the pain reached the level at which it can't be tolerated anymore. The point at which frustration and anger become a promise to yourself that the pain of taking action can not be nearly as bad as the pain of the status quo.

How To Run a Company Like Jason Fried


Photo by: Sergey Nivens

Last week I went to see Jason Fried, the co-founder of 37 Signals, give a talk about their ideology and reasons for their success. Here are some things he shared:

Be patient. We see companies like Facebook, Twitter or Instagram achieve overnight success (or what looks like overnight success), and believe that is the norm, while in reality, those companies are lottery winners. Most companies grow at a much slower rate.

Do not raise money. Bootstrap. This will teach you how to make money, instead of how to spend money. Furthermore, you will have the freedom to make decisions and control the timeline without the pressure from investors who want their money back quickly.

Focus on the long term. The companies that stay in business for a long time are automatically successful. They survived, which means they found something that works. After being in the industry for such a long time, their experience helps them make better decisions.

What will you customers or clients want in 10 years, but also want it now? Amazon customers won't ever say: "I wish that shipping took longer or was more expensive."

Focus on what matters, and focus on it now.

Hire remote workers. In some industries, this may not be possible, but in software development, there is no reason for a company to miss on the best talent available just because they aren't in the 30 mile radius. Similarly, the employee should be able to live wherever he or she wants and work for a company of choice.

Marketing is not a department. Marketing is everything you do. At 37 Signals, marketing is everybody's responsibility. Every blog post and tweet is obviously marketing, as well as every communication with a client and the product that you create.

Culture is not what you say you do, but what you end up doing. It is not what should be, but what is.

Evaluate people based on the value they produce, and not by how many hours they put in.

The best way to motivate employees is to give them work they find meaningful. This will motivate them intrinsically and make them productive and loyal. Somebody else can always give them more money or offer better perks, so don't rely on these extrinsic motivational factors (but do pay above market rates).

If you have an idea you would like to work on, do it! Start small and see how it goes.
A few hours a week may be enough. That way, you can give your project a shot, but if it doesn't work out, it won't be too big of a deal.

Have Strong Enough Whys


Photo by: Robert Byron

If you feel like you need to do something, or that you should do something, but don't really want to, there is a conflict inside of you.

One part of you thinks it's best for you to go ahead and do the task, but another part resists that thought, and wants to do something else (or nothing at all).

Be honest with yourself. Why do you think you should do it? What happens if you don't? Free yourself up from this obligation and convince yourself that you have a choice. If you don't do anything, will the world come to an end? If you don't take action, what's going to happen tomorrow? What about a week later? A month later? Year, 5 years? Will the pain grow in intensity, or will it vanish? What will happen to the reason you are doing this in the first place?

Now let's switch things a little. What if you do take action? What pain will you endure? How long will this pain last? Is this pain long lasting, or is it just temporary? What is it exactly that's stopping you from taking action?

Keep asking yourself why, and drill deeper and deeper with each question. Get to the bottom of this. Tell yourself the truth, no matter what.
