How To Master Any Skill

I'm a Toastmasters member. For those that don't know, Toasmasters is a club whose purpose is improving your public speaking skills.

When making any type of decision, as part of the process, we commonly identify the return on investment. We evaluate the benefits, risks and investments to see if the commitment is worth the prize.

Mastering public speaking has many benefits. Public speaking develops your ability to communicate things clearly and concisely, not just when speaking to a group of people, but also in a one-on-one conversations.

It also improves your self-confidence. The Toastmasters club is a friendly and supportive environment, but still, before every speech, you get nervous. You start doubting yourself. "What if I mess up and embarass myself?"

Especially Table Topics. If you don't know how Table Topics work, this can be a little shocking to you: You are supposed to give a short speech without any preparation.

Scary, right?

The Table Topic Master gives you a topic and after that you have only a few seconds to think about it before you start speaking. Sometimes you are familiar with the topic and you have thought (or maybe even talked) about it before. But other times, you need to do all the thinking in those few seconds.

Keep in mind that knowing what to talk about is not all there is to it. You need to think about how you are going to express yourself, while observing the audience and taking their non-verbal feedback into account.

This teaches you to focus.

Furthermore, to be able to deliver an impromptu speech succesfully, realize that nervousness can be converted to positive energy. This approach builds your confidence and self esteem. It gets easier every time.

What's also very interesting is that when doing a table topic for the first time, a lot of people do a great job, but when talking to them afterwards, they think they sucked.

Instead, be proud of yourself for overcoming the fear.

One of the most important lessons you learn at Toastmasters is how to deal with fear. The fear loses its power when you realize that you speak in public all the time anyway. You talk to people at work, your family and your friends, and you don't fret over what you're going to say next or what's going to happen if you say something stupid.

In concluding this blog post, I'd like to ask you to realize that you may apply this strategy to the areas other than public speaking, as well.

1. Find a low risk environment and commit to practicing the skill on a consistent basis

2. Prepare the best you can, and find excitement in the things you can't predict

3. Learn from other people's feedback and self-reflection

Now pick a skill you would want to master and give it a try. Good luck!

We Are All Under Pressure


Photo by: Sergey Nivens

A single woman with no kids in her late 20s who is constantly asked "Have you met anybody?" or "When are you going to have kids?" is under a lot of pressure. So is a man who worked hard to save up enough money and now wants to take a risk of quitting his day job and starting his own company.

Family and friends are well intentioned. They want you to be happy. But the problem is that their expectations might not match yours.

Most people offer advice based on their values, not yours. Instead of trying to understand you and support you, they want to tell you what they would do if they were in your situation, ignoring your individual needs and beliefs.

This lack of perspective on their side makes the existing process of going through a change (or not wanting to go through a change) more difficult and painful.

So how do you deal with it?

1. Get clear on what you want and stick with it. Believe in yourself. Be patient.

2. Surround yourself with supportive people.

3. Don't worry too much about people who don't get you. If they truly care about you, they will respect your decisions regardless, since all they really want to see at the end of the day, is you being happy.

Pain Is a Teacher


Photo by: Ship Factory

You feel pain for a reason: Your body is letting you know that something needs to be changed. It makes you aware of a problem.

Often times, the pain teaches you a lesson.

If you try to avoid pain, you may never learn the lesson. I'm not talking just about physical pain. Emotional pain can be avoided as well, by distracting yourself or using drugs or alcohol.

Sometimes people learn the wrong lesson. I tore the ACL in my right knee playing basketball - maybe I should've never played? If a woman gets cheated on, are all men unfaithful? If you are not happy with your past, should you give up on your future?

Pain can make you a better person. You may know some great people who experienced a lot of pain in their lives. It is much easier for you to understand how another person feels if you've felt that way before. Be careful, though, every situation is different - don't assume too much too early.

The saying goes: "If it doesn't kill you, it makes you stronger." If you've experienced a tremendeous amount of pain in the past, the present and future might look less scary, because the new pain will be insignificant in comparison to the old pain.

The alternative is to get scared of the additional pain or start feeling helpless. You decide.

Pain can be a very strong driving force. Have you ever said to yourself (or heard someone else say) in frustration: "I've had enough!" At that point, the pain reached the level at which it can't be tolerated anymore. The point at which frustration and anger become a promise to yourself that the pain of taking action can not be nearly as bad as the pain of the status quo.
